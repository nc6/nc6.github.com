The Game of Hells part 2

Posted on October 18, 2013 by Nick

I’ve been thinking some more about the game of Hells introduced in a previous post, and how to do better than the simple strategy we introduced there.

So, whilst I initially thought it inelegant, the approach of paring down the possibilities seems like a good one. However, simply picking the first remaining possibility is unlikely to be the best approach. What we’d like is a way of choosing the next guess to give us the best hope of cutting down the remaining possibilities. Before we get to this, however, let’s introduce some terminology.

Let H be the current history of a game (recall that the history is the series of moves leading up to that point). Define ΩH to be the set of all possible moves remaining given a history H. Now for any move m ∈ ΩH, we have a set of possible responses I (from 0 cows, 0 bulls up to 4 bulls), and each response i gives rise to a set Rg, i ⊆ ΩH of those elements that score i against g. In other words, Rg, i represents the possibilities that would remain should we get response i to guess g.

We call the set Rg = {Rg, i  :  i ∈ I} the residuals of g. Let ng, i = |Rg, i| be the size of i-th residual. Then we may define the following three strategies:

  1. The optimistic strategy. Choose g = argming{mini {ng, i}}.
  2. The pessimistic stragegy. Choose g = argming{maxi {ng, i}}.
  3. The cynical strategy. Choose g = argming{(∑ing, i)/|I|}.

The optimistic strategy wants to believe that every move it makes is going to respond with the best answer (obviously, we exclude the 4 bulls case!) possible, the one that gives it the most information, and so it heavily gambles on these outcomes. I suspect it will do quite badly, at least in early turns.

The pessimistic strategy thinks that every guess it makes will be terrible, and tries to minimise the damage. The cynical strategy tries to game the system.

It seems a big heavyweight to calculate residual class sizes for every possible move, however. What we’d like is a way to reduce the number of candidates we consider. One way to do this would be to prove beforehand that the residual class sizes for two possibilities were going to be identical. Let  ∼  be defined as follows: for a, b ∈ ΩH, we say that a ∼ b iff ∀i (na, i = nb, i). It should be straightforward that  ∼  is an equivalence relation on ΩH, and that we need only check one member of each equivalence class in order to determine a suitable candidate guess g.

Now, also for a, b ∈ ΩH we say that a and b are indistinguishable under H if, given the transposition πa, b which swaps a and b, there is some permutation of moves σ such that πa, b(H) = σ(H). In other words, a and b are indistinguishable if swapping them in every move results in a mere reordering of the history.

Consider the following sequence of moves (clearly non-optimal):

  1. [0,1,2,3] - 0 cows, 1 bull
  2. [0,4,5,6] - 2 cows, 1 bull
  3. [0,5,4,6] - 2 cows, 1 bull

Then 7 and 8 are clearly indistinguishable since they do not occur in the history. Likewise, 4 and 5 are indistinguishable since exchanging them will result in a history involving swapping step 2 and step 3.
